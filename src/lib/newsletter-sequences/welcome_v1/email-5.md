Most multi-unit operators I talk to think in two modes: STR or LTR.

I think in three: STR, exchange-student-rental, LTR. And the right unit might do all three across one calendar year.

Here's how my 6 units actually map:

---

**Unit A — Center 2BR, downtown Riga.**
STR May–September ({{str_summer_rate_typical}}/night, ~70% occupancy). Flips to a furnished student-rental Oct–April at {{exchange_student_rent_typical}}/month + utilities. Highest annual yield in the portfolio.

**Unit B — Old Town studio.**
Year-round STR. Tourists in winter too because it's literally in the medieval city center. The exception, not the rule.

**Unit C — Mežaparks 1BR.**
Year-round student rental. Near a university. Quiet residential. Was never going to be a strong STR — neighborhood doesn't have the foot traffic. Permanent flip.

**Unit D — Pārdaugava 3BR.**
Year-round LTR. Family-friendly side of the river. Lower yield per square meter than STR would give in summer, but zero turnover, zero management overhead, predictable income.

**Unit E — Jūrmala studio.**
The unit I lost {{loss_per_unit_per_season}} on last winter. SHOULD do STR May–Sept (high tourist demand) and student rental Oct–April. This year it will.

**Unit F — Imanta 2BR.**
Year-round LTR. Working-class district. Steady demand from working professionals on the bus line. Doesn't justify STR overhead at all.

---

The decision tree I run on each unit:

- If summer STR yield > 2× LTR-equivalent → flip seasonally
- If 1.6×–2× → consider permanent student-rental year-round (the Mežaparks pattern)
- If < 1.6× → just do LTR, don't fight it

Most "should I do STR or LTR" debates are bad framing. The real question is **which mix this unit, in this neighborhood, in this season.**
