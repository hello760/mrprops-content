Most STR content pushes more STR.

I'm going to push the opposite for this email.

Some of your units shouldn't be on Airbnb at all. Not seasonally. Not partially. Not "let me try it for a quarter and see." Just LTR, year-round, forever.

---

Two of my 6 units fit this:

**Unit D, Pārdaugava 3BR.** Family-friendly riverside neighborhood. Quiet streets. Schools nearby. Three bedrooms — too big for the typical Airbnb 2-night-couple, too far from Old Town for the tourist crowd. Could rent on Airbnb. Wouldn't yield enough to justify the turnover overhead. So it's LTR at {{ltr_rent_typical}}/mo to a family that's been there 2 years.

**Unit F, Imanta 2BR.** Working-class district. No tourist demand. Solid LTR demand from working professionals on the bus line. Works as LTR at {{ltr_rent_typical}}/mo. Wouldn't clear half that on STR.

---

The math I run:

If `(annual STR gross) ÷ (12 × monthly LTR rent)` is less than **1.6**, the unit shouldn't be STR.

Why 1.6? Because operational overhead — cleaning, supplies, listing management, seasonal rate adjustments, dealing with the occasional bad guest — eats roughly 30-40% of the gross margin. So a 1.6× ratio nets you roughly the same as plain LTR, but with way more hours spent.

If you're at 1.0–1.6× you're literally working unpaid hours for no margin gain.

---

How to know which units this is:

Pull last 12 months of STR gross revenue per unit. Divide by 12. Compare to what your local LTR rate would be (Zillow / Idealista / Booking Long-stay / your local equivalent).

If the ratio is below 1.6, run the LTR projection. If LTR equals or exceeds STR in expected annual return, flip it permanently.

The peace-of-mind dividend (no calendar to manage, no cleaner coordination, no review anxiety) is the bonus you don't see on the spreadsheet.
